A friend showed me his memory architecture. Here's how it compares to workingset.

Two systems with almost identical vocabulary arrived at different layers of the same problem. The interesting parts are where they don't overlap.

A friend sent me his design for a "Tiered Decay Memory System" for an LLM agent. He asked what the difference is between his system and workingset, the markdown-vault context compactor I shipped last week, and whether either of us could learn anything from the other. The honest answer is that we arrived at almost the exact same vocabulary independently — Hot state, Warm index, Cold archive, nightly script with no LLM — but we built it at different layers. His operates on the agent's runtime memory. Mine operates on the source corpus. They compose more naturally than they compete.

This is the side-by-side I sent him, lightly cleaned up and stripped of identifying details so it's safe to post publicly. If you're building anything that needs to be smart about what the model sees on each turn, the differences between these two designs are the interesting part. The mistakes I think we both should worry about live in the gap between them.

tl;dr

His system answers: how does a single agent manage its own memory across a long session? Role separation (Doer / Librarian / Retriever), tiered decay, active curation by a cheap classifier.

Mine answers: how do I cram a 325K-token hand-curated markdown vault into a 200K context window without losing the important parts? Deterministic extraction, BM25, measurement-first.

The vocabulary lines up almost row-for-row, but the layer is different. Workingset can plug in as the Cold-archive substrate underneath his Librarian, and his Librarian could give workingset the runtime hook it doesn't have today. Neither replaces the other.

Two systems, side by side

friend's design

Tiered Decay Memory

A live three-role system that classifies and decays memory tier-by-tier as the agent runs.

  • Doer Opus / Sonnet · runs task, emits receipt
  • Librarian Haiku · classifies, serial
  • Retriever Haiku · sub-agent for gap-fill
  • Hot 2 KB cap · loads every task
  • Warm keyword pointers · scored
  • Cold append-only · explicit fetch
  • Dead audit-only flag
  • Nightly no LLM · decay + rescore
workingset

Vault-aware context compactor

A deterministic CLI that turns a markdown vault into one bounded brief per branch, plus BM25 retrieval on demand.

  • Doer any agent · reads brief.md
  • Curator deterministic Python · no LLM
  • Query ws query --budget N · BM25
  • Brief ~8 KB cap · loads every task
  • Index SQLite FTS5 · scored
  • Vault markdown on disk · explicit fetch
  • Compact stacked STATUS → sidecar
  • Cron nightly ws brief --write

Read those two columns top to bottom. Same vocabulary, almost row-for-row. We arrived at the same shape independently, which is itself the most interesting thing about the comparison — it suggests this is the shape the problem actually has, not a coincidence of taste. The interesting differences are what's a model and what's deterministic.

What each system has that the other doesn't

What his has that mine doesn't

  • Active runtime curation by a cheap model. My curator is Python rules at brief-generation time. His reacts to what just happened.
  • Explicit receipt loop at end of task. A first-class hook I don't have. Mine assumes nightly is good enough.
  • Dead layer as first-class audit trail. Mine ends up in git history — implicit, not queryable.
  • A decay scoring formula. Recency × frequency × pin bonus. I have implicit recency only.
  • Role separation as the architectural invariant. "Doer never writes. Librarian never reasons. Script never calls an LLM." Clean. Mine doesn't have a Doer/Librarian split because it doesn't have a Librarian.

What mine has that his doesn't

  • Empirical measurement built in. ws diff --tokenizer tiktoken gives real before/after numbers. His spec has no measurement step.
  • A multi-run hallucination benchmark. 7 models × 3 reps on a near-miss-number trap. Tells you which models you can trust to abstain.
  • Vault-shape awareness. Knows about branches, STATUS blocks, frontmatter, checkboxes. His is vault-agnostic — more general, but he'll have to invent extraction rules per corpus.
  • Deterministic extraction. Reproducible. Parallelizable. No serial bottleneck. His Librarian is explicitly serial — that's his throughput cap.
  • A shipping OSS tool, validated at scale. v0.4.0, 31 real branches, 25.6× aggregate compression, agent-installer wired in.

Three concrete things he could lift from workingset

I told him I'd be uninterested if the answer was just "we're solving different problems, good luck." So here's the actually-useful part: three specific things he could borrow that would tighten his design measurably.

Benefit 1 · validation harness

Run a 3-run test on his Librarian before he commits a cheap model to that role.

His Librarian uses a small classifier model, and its job is classification near a decision boundary: enter, push, or flag dead. My benchmark found small classifier models hallucinated on near-miss-number trap questions 33% of the time — one run abstained correctly, one fabricated an answer, one half-fabricated. Same model, same prompt, three different outputs.

If his Librarian misclassifies an enter as flag dead one time in three, his audit trail eats the decision permanently. The bench/ folder in the workingset repo is a copy-able 2-model minimal harness — point it at his Librarian's classification task and he'll know within an hour whether the cheap model is safe in that role, or whether the cost delta to a bigger model is worth it.

Benefit 2 · the Cold-archive substrate, for free

If his Cold archive is markdown (or could be), drop workingset under it.

His Cold archive is "where stuff lives, append-only, explicit fetch." That is exactly what workingset does at the markdown layer: BM25 retrieval plus deterministic brief generation, with no LLM in the curation loop.

Wire it in and his Librarian curates over a much smaller surface — the 8 KB brief, not the whole vault. His Retriever sub-agent stops needing to be an LLM at all for keyword gap-fill (just ws query --budget N), which leaves the cheap classifier model free for the cases that actually need reasoning. Same architecture, lower cost, fewer places for hallucination to slip in.

Benefit 3 · the measurement discipline

Add a ws diff-shaped measurement step to his spec: source-tokens / brief-tokens / ratio.

The single most useful thing the workingset workflow gave me was the habit of running ws diff --tokenizer tiktoken before trusting the compactor. The before-number (325K tokens for a real vault) is the one you'd never have guessed. The after-number (5K) is the proof. The ratio (64.5×) is the receipt.

His design as drawn has no equivalent step. Adding one — "after a Librarian sweep, what's the Hot and Warm token cost, and what was it before?" — gives him a regression signal the moment a decay change makes things worse. Without it, he's flying blind on the thing the architecture is supposed to optimize.

Three things I'd lift from his design in return

The borrowing isn't one-directional. There are three things in his diagram that workingset doesn't have today, and at least two of them belong in the next version.

  • The receipt-at-end-of-task hook. Workingset's nightly cron is fine for stable vaults, but agents that produce structured task outputs would benefit from a more reactive curator. A receipt schema (decisions, supersedes, open threads, importance) is a real design choice I haven't made yet.
  • First-class Dead layer. I track superseded decisions in git history, which is implicit and not queryable. Promoting "this was the answer until X" to a first-class flag in the index would let an agent answer "what did we decide six months ago, and what overrode it" without spelunking through git log.
  • Role separation as an invariant. His "Doer never writes, Librarian never reasons, Script never calls an LLM" is a clean architectural rule that prevents whole classes of bugs. Workingset has no equivalent invariant because there is no Librarian. If I add one, I want the invariant first.

but the dollar number is the wrong story

Reading those tables as "workingset is infinitely cheaper" is a misread. We chose different things. I bought $0 maintenance with hand-written extraction rules — the cost is my time when the vault shape changes and the regex misses something new. He bought adaptivity with $0.005/task — his Librarian handles vault shapes I'd need code changes for, and reacts to what just happened. Neither approach dominates without knowing the deployment shape.

The real costs in his design aren't the dollars

The dollar bill is the easy thing to see. The actual costs of his design are three things that don't show up on an invoice:

  1. Serial bottleneck. "Librarian = serial" caps task throughput at Haiku's classification latency (~1-2 seconds). Fine at 500 tasks/day. At 10K tasks/day across an agent fleet, the Librarian queue becomes a real engineering problem he has to design around — work-stealing, sharded librarians, batching. That's weeks of work, not pennies of API spend.
  2. Classification error rate. My benchmark found Haiku 4.5 misclassified near-the-boundary trap questions 33% of the time. If one-in-three of his flag dead decisions misfires, his audit trail is unreliable in the exact spots that matter most — superseded decisions near a boundary. That cost shows up months later as "wait, why did we say X six months ago?" The right answer was there, then the Librarian flagged it dead, and now nobody can find it.
  3. Compounding context cost. Every Librarian call reads the receipt plus the current Hot state. As Hot state grows toward its 2 KB cap, the per-call input creeps up and the bill creeps with it. Mine doesn't have this — the brief is regenerated nightly from scratch, so size is bounded by construction.

The framing I'd actually offer him

honest framing

Mine is free at runtime but rigid at write-time — I pay in code maintenance when a vault has a new shape. His is ~$0.005/task at runtime but adaptive — he pays in Librarian-serial-throughput, classification error rate, and ongoing LLM bills. For a single-user single-vault scenario where the vault shape is stable (my exact case), mine wins on cost and correctness. For a multi-tenant agent service with diverse vault shapes evolving fast, his wins on adaptivity and the dollar cost is small relative to the deployment.

The two systems are pricing two different things. The right comparison isn't "which is cheaper" — it's "which one's cost model matches your deployment."

One concrete ask if he ships the prototype: instrument the Librarian's calls and log two numbers — actual $/task and classification-flip rate when the same receipt is replayed. Both are cheap to collect, and together they tell him whether his architecture is paying for itself or quietly bleeding correctness.

The honest pushback

If you're going to send a friend a comparison post, the comparison is hollow if it doesn't include the parts you'd push back on. The three I sent him directly:

probe · Librarian as bottleneck and risk

"Librarian = cheap model + serial" is both a throughput cap and a correctness risk. Serial is the system's bottleneck under load. A cheap classifier at 33% near-miss-misclassification means the audit trail isn't trustworthy at the boundary. Two mitigations: (a) keep Dead reversible (he already does — good), (b) sample 100 Librarian decisions, re-run them through a bigger model, count the diffs. If the diff rate is low, the cheap model is fine. If it's not, he knows the cost of cheap classification.

probe · "task" is doing a lot of work

"Receipt at the end of every task" — what's a task, operationally? A turn? A tool-call cluster? A user message? A conversation? The choice changes how often the Librarian runs, which changes its serial-bottleneck cost. Worth pinning down before implementation.

probe · decay weights aren't in the diagram

The decay formula has recency, frequency, and pin terms. These terms are different magnitudes. The weights are doing most of the work and aren't shown. Make them explicit, then tune them empirically (see Benefit 3 above) rather than guessing.

the honest no-equivalent gap

What I genuinely don't have that he does: an agent that learns from itself. Workingset assumes a human writes good markdown and a machine compresses it. His Librarian assumes the agent's own task-outputs are the source of truth and curates them live. That's a more ambitious bet. It's also where most of the unsolved problems live: classification drift, decay miscalibration, lost-context recovery. Worth knowing what you're signing up for.

The takeaway

If you've been building anything in this space, the lesson buried in this comparison is the one worth taking. Two engineers, working independently, with no shared design doc, both arrived at the same five-layer shape: a bounded hot tier loaded every task, a keyword-indexed warm tier, an append-only cold tier, a flagged-dead audit tier, and a deterministic nightly maintenance step. We disagreed on what should be a model and what should be code, but we agreed on the shape.

That kind of convergence usually means the structure of the problem is doing the talking. If you're starting from scratch, you can probably skip the intermediate steps both of us took to get here and start from this shape directly.

The remaining questions are the interesting ones: where in the stack does the cheap LLM go, what fraction of curation has to be reactive vs nightly, and what's your trap-question hallucination rate when you actually measure it. Those are the parts that don't fall out of the architecture, and they're the parts that decide whether the thing actually works.
